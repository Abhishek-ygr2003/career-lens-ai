Problem 2: The "5 pages" limitation

This is a classic crawler problem.

You probably don't actually need every single job every day.

Think like a data engineer.

Option A — Rolling collection (recommended)

Example:

Monday:
Data Science
Naukri pages 1-20

Tuesday:
Software Engineering
Naukri pages 1-20

Wednesday:
Cybersecurity
Naukri pages 1-20

Then repeat with Foundit.

Over a week:

Naukri
+
Foundit
+
Adzuna
+
5 domains
+
many keywords

You slowly build a large dataset.

Problem 3: Job lifecycle management

This is the biggest design challenge.

Your idea:

Remove closed jobs but keep historical counts

is exactly the right direction.

Never physically delete data.

You need a job lifecycle.

Your jobs table should have something like:

job_id

source

source_job_id

first_seen

last_seen

is_active

closed_at

collection_count

Example:

Day 1:

Google
Data Scientist

first_seen:
June 1

last_seen:
June 1

active:
True

Day 15:

Still exists:

last_seen:
June 15

active:
True

Day 30:

Not found anymore:

active:
False

closed_at:
June 30

But you keep the record.

Because now your analytics can answer:

How many Data Scientist jobs were posted in June?
Which companies hired the most in 2026?
Which fields are growing month-over-month?
Average job lifetime?

This is very valuable data.

Problem 6: Rate limits and anti-bot

This is where your architecture matters.

Do not do:

for source in sources:
    for domain in domains:
        for keyword in keywords:
            scrape 100 pages

You will get blocked.

Better:

Task Queue


Naukri + Data Scientist page 1

wait

Foundit + Data Scientist page 1

wait

Adzuna + Data Scientist page 1

wait

Spread requests over time.
